Covid Misinformation Background

Nowadays, fake news is a pressing issue especially in social media platforms. According to a statistics portal that collates information from diverse topics called Statista, about 34% of North Amercans trust the information they find on social media platforms. Unfortunately, social media platforms are the breeding ground for fake news. In fact, about 31% of children and teenagers share fake news online.

Indeed, the issue itself has a significant impact amongst the community. About a decent portion of Americans believe that fake news leads to a great deal of confusion. Aside from confusion, it causes an unnecessary panic shown through mass toilet paper buying and hoarding of other essential goods at home.

In addition, the misinformation in our community leads to a false sense of complacency. For instance, a handful of people went to the beach on the 4th of July. Hence, fake news is an alarming issue that should be addressed in the community to avoid its dangerous effects.

As teenagers we wanted to see how much our particular demographic contributes to this issue, and how we can better identify misinformation.

Inspiration and Purpose

This data analysis project is inspired by the current prevalence of misinformation amongst selected Twitter communities to spread awareness about this issue.

Ideally, the website exhibits the collected data through visual representations such as bar charts and pie graphs. The website contains graphs that display data about each individual twitter group and graphs that compares results between groups.
