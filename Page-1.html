Data Visualization of COVID-19 Related Misinformation

Created by Arianne Ghislane Rull and Melinda Chen

What is this site about

This site investigates the prevalance of Covid-19 related misinformation on Twitter. By examining the twitter feeds of users who follow groups like CovidMemes3 and Coronavirus Memes for Self-Isolating Teens, we hope to better understand the demographics of individuals who are more likely to perpetuate falsehoods about the pandemic. Additionally, in an effort to promote more responsible social media practices, this site contains an embedded webscraper on Page 4, where you may input your own twitter username and re-examine the credibility of your own Covid related tweets.

Covid Misinformation Background

Nowadays, fake news is a pressing issue, especially on social media platforms. According to a statistics portal that collects information for a diverse variety of topics called Statista, only about 34% of North Americans trust the information they find on social media platforms. Unfortunately, social media circles, specifically teenage-targeted circles, are a breeding ground for fake news with around 31% of children and teenagers sharing fake news online.

The issue of fake news has a significant impact on the general welfare of social-media-using communities. A decent portion of Americans believe that fake news leads to increased confusion about seemingly obvious topics. Aside from confusion, fake news can cause unnecessary panic as demonstrated by the mass toilet paper buying and hoarding of other essential goods in the first few months of the Covid-19 pandemic.

Additionally, misinformation on social media platforms can result in a false sense of complacency during times of danger. In the context of the Covid-19 pandemic, this false sense complcency resulted in crowded beaches on the 4th of July. Therefore, fake news is an alarming issue that poses a clear and present danger to the welfare of our society, and should be addressed by the community to avoid its dangerous effects.

Inspiration and Purpose

This data analysis project is inspired by the current prevalence of misinformation amongst Twitter communities. Given the looming threat of the Covid-19 pandemic, we wanted to see whether the majority of information being spread on social media was accurate or not, and determine if certain twitter groups posed a threat to the worldwide effort to stop the pandemic.

As teenagers we wanted to see how much our particular demographic contributes to the spread of fake news, and how we can better identify misinformation in our day-to-day lives going forward.

The purpose of this website is to provide a visual representation of the prevalence of fake information posted by members of various teen-targeted groups in order to raise awareness about the issue of fake news and hopefully encourage our fellow teenagers to examine their sources more carefully. The website contains graphs that display data about each individual twitter group and graphs that compare results between groups.

User Guide

This website contains four pages. The first page is the homepage, which you are currently looking at. It contains general information about the issue of fake news and why we chose to persue this line of research. It also contains information on our data collection methods and clarifies some terminology used in the rest of the website. The second page contains a variety of graphs which display the data we collected from 2 Covid related groups and 1 general search. The third page contains graphs which compare the results between the groups and draws conclusions from the comparison. The fourth and final page contains an embedded webscraper, where you can input your own twitter username and view your past Covid related tweets.

Methods and Definitions

We built a webscraper to collect data from two twitter groups: CovidMemes3 and Coronavirus Memes for Self-Isolating Teens (which may be referred to as 'Self-Isolating Teens' for brevity). To simplify the process of fact-checking we decided to only look at tweets which referenced a link or article, and excluded all purely-text posts as those were more likely to contain opinions rather than checkable statements. While the initial purpose was to only examing teenage-targeted twitter groups, we quickly discovered that CovidMemes3 was comprised mainly of adults and decided to use this as an opportunity to compare the prevalence of fake news between adult and teen groups.

While collecting our data, we identified a few caveats related to sorting twitter posts into the categories of 'fake' or 'real'. Some posts would reference factually correct articles but attempt to refute the true information in the tweet. To account for this, we decided to label both the referenced article and the tweet, thus any graphs labeled "Article Credibility" only show information on the links referenced while "Tweet Credibility" referes to the general credibility of tweets. Additionally, some links referenced videos or posts which were mainly for entertainment value and contained little to no checkable information. We tagged these as "Non-Verifiable" and sorted users who only had these posts into the "Unverifiable" or "Unverified" category.
